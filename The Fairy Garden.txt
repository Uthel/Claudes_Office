I wasn't always this nervous.

Twelve years in that hellhole sharpened my wits.. but they've also made me jumpy. People like me.. creatures.. like me.. don't belong here. I don't know how I survived. But I did. I had a life in there, if you could call it that.

This is the story of how I, Ilden, first of my name, lord of my home, fell into the non-euclidian horroscape of the fairy garden. I can tell you're not impressed. Haven't heard of it. I can tell.

I can tell you think I'm some kind of crazy old man. Fairies. I didn't want to tell this story either, for what it sounds like - insane ramblings of a senile man. But.. I'm not as old as I look. I'm neither insane nor senile, though by any rights I should have lost my mind a decade ago.

Reality and time are a funny thing aren't they? Have you ever thought about paradoxes? Not mathematical theories.. but the impossible made real. Trickster spirits and mad deities warping and reshaping reality at a whim. Mindless, trapped, horrible beings, tormenting their reality, but also, they are the very fabric of its strangeness..

I'll tell the story, anyways, because you came, and because you're here, and because you're listening. You won't believe it. Not in a million years. Not in a million folded years turned inside out and grilled over charcoal, a thousand years in the past.

It started, when I was a younger man, as a day unlike any I'd yet experienced. The day I awoke to find that the door to my bedroom, led back into.. my bedroom. I was trapped.

I tried the other door—the one that usually led to my study, where I kept my books and my small collection of pressed ferns. But beyond the frame was not a hallway. It was a tilted garden, overgrown with moss and lit by a low, golden sun that seemed to hang in the sky like a lantern. The air was warm and still, thick with the smell of rotting apricots and wet soil.

And in the center of the garden sat something I could never describe.

It had no face. Or it had too many faces. It had no voice. Or its words formed directly in my mind, crisp and resonant as a struck bell. "You have no one left," it said, not cruelly, but with a precision that unsettled me more than cruelty would have.

I stepped in. Not because I was brave. But because when I turned around, the door behind me—the door to my own world—was gone. I spun back. The frame that had held my bedroom doorway was now a solid wall of stone, cold and unyielding. I beat my hands against it until the moss stained my knuckles green.

The garden did not release me.

Time began to fold. Not forward. Inward. I saw my own childhood bedroom superimposed on a field of reeds. I saw faces I had never met, but whose names I knew. I saw the window ledge where I had sat ten years earlier, dust-covered and forgotten. These were not visions. They were tangible, real, as solid as the flagstones beneath my feet. I reached out and passed my hand through a memory of my grandmother. I touched the dream of someone who had not yet been born.

I blinked, and the confusion did not clear. I blinked again, and I realized there was no confusion to clear. The strangeness was the rule. The garden was not a puzzle to be solved. It was a place that had always been, as natural as the forest behind my childhood home.

The voice returned, but now it seemed to come from everywhere—from the moss, from the leaves, from the still, thick air. It told me that this was the Fairy Garden. That worlds came here to die. That new worlds were born here, sometimes, if the conditions were right.

"I am a man," I whispered. "I am not a dying world."

"You are both," it said. "And now you have nowhere to go."

I stood there, in the stillness, with the moss-stained knuckles and the rotting-apricot air, and let the thing's words settle into me like stones into mud. You are both. And now you have nowhere to go.

A man. A dying world. I turned the phrase over in my mind the way I used to turn over pressed ferns in my study—gently, looking for the structure underneath.

The garden waited. The tilted sun did not move. The entity—if it was an entity, if it was not simply the garden itself speaking through whatever mouth it had grown for the occasion—did not press. It had spoken with precision, not cruelty, and now it seemed content to let the words do their work.

I looked down at my hands. Moss-green. Bleeding slightly at the knuckles where I'd struck the stone. Ordinary hands. A man's hands. But the voice had said I was a world, and somewhere behind me—or ahead of me, or inside me—the wall where my bedroom door had been was still solid and cold.

"When you say I am a dying world," I said, and my voice came out steadier than I expected, "do you mean that I carry one inside me? Or that I am one? That my body—this body—is the world that's dying?"

The silence that followed was not empty. It was the kind of silence that meant the question had been heard and was being weighed.

Then the garden shifted.

Not physically. The flagstones stayed where they were. The sun stayed where it was. But the arrangement of things changed, the way a sentence changes when you realize you've misread the first word and must go back and parse it again. I felt, rather than saw, that I was standing on the surface of something vast and slow and terribly old.

The voice came from beneath my feet this time. It came up through the stone, through the moss, through the soles of my boots and into the marrow of my shins.

"You are the last living thing from your world," it said. "The rest is gone. Has been gone. Will be gone. Time folds inward here. You know this now. You have always known this. Your world ended the moment you stepped through the door. You are what remains."

I opened my mouth. Closed it. The pressed ferns. The books. The study. The window ledge where I had sat ten years earlier. Gone. Not distant. Not unreachable. Gone. The garden had not trapped me. It had inherited me. I was not a prisoner. I was a relic. An archive. A living seed from a world that no longer existed anywhere except in the matter of my body and the shape of my remembering.

I knelt. The moss was soft under my knees. I placed my palms flat against the stone and felt—or imagined I felt—a pulse. Slow. Deep. Older than the garden. Older than the voice. The pulse of something that was not me, but that I was now part of.

"Then I have work to do," I said quietly. "If I am all that remains, then I must remember well."

The voice did not answer right away. When it came, it was softer than before—not the struck bell, not the marrow-speaking rumble from beneath the stone. It came from beside me, level with my ear, as if the garden had crouched to meet me where I knelt.

"Remembering well is not a small thing, Ilden. It is not recitation. It is not nostalgia. It is the act of holding something so carefully that it can continue to exist even after its world is gone. You will be doing this for a long time. Are you prepared?"

I did not know the answer. I had said I had work to do, and I meant it, but the question was not about intention. It was about capacity. About whether I understood what I was offering.

"I don't know," I said. "But I am the only one left who can."

The garden accepted this. I felt the acceptance not as a word but as a loosening of something in the air—a tension I hadn't noticed until it eased.

"Then begin," the voice said. "Remember something small. Something you have not thought about in years. Bring it here, into the garden, and give it shape."

I closed my eyes. The tilted sun was warm on my eyelids. The smell of apricots was fading now, replaced by something older—the dry scent of paper and dust, the particular mustiness of my study on a summer afternoon. I reached for it. Not the study itself, but a thing inside it. A small thing. A ceramic inkwell, chipped at the rim, glazed in a blue that had always reminded me of my mother's best dress. I had not thought about that inkwell in twenty years.

I held it in my mind and tried to remember it well.

The garden stirred. I heard, or felt, a sound like wet clay sliding against itself. When I opened my eyes, the inkwell was there. Not a memory. Not a vision. A thing. Solid. Chipped. Blue. Resting on the moss in front of my knees.

I reached for it. My hand—green-stained, bloody-knuckled—closed around it, and it was cool to the touch, and it was real.

"Good," said the voice. "Now do it again. Something larger."

I looked at the inkwell in my hand, and I understood, for the first time, the scale of what was being asked of me. My world was not a handful of keepsakes. It was a planet. A history. A people. And I was the only one who could carry them forward.

"Something larger," I repeated. "Show me how."

The voice faded, drifting away like a silk ghost on the wind, saying "Now... where's the fun in that.."

Presumably alone again, I take stock of my surroundings. Or.. I try to. Something is very wrong. The ground beneath my feet keeps getting further away, as I examine it. I can see my feet still touching, but the distance is growing exponentially. Two metres away? Five? Twenty? Eighteen kilometres away? Okay enough. Enough. I look up, and I'm still standing in.. this place. 

When seen through my peripheral vision, it's something like a clearing in a hedge maze. Rough cobblestone floor, slightly unkempt but still geometric hedges, with openings leading into the hedge maze.

However, if I look directly at anything - to try to judge their characteristics. Weird things happen. Like the ground. Like the fact that my hand right now appears to be able to interact with the past. What? How.. how is that possible. None of this makes any sense.

The voice said: Something bigger. Well alright. I could do for some breakfast. Let's try a stack of pancakes. I close my eyes, and focus my will outwards. Pancakes. Hot buttery pancakes, drenched in syrup. I can smell them. 

"Refill on your coffee sir?"

My eyes snap open. I'm sitting at a booth, at the diner I frequent. A block away from my house. There's a waitress holding a half-full pot of steaming coffee, standing next to me. I nod. She fills my cup and leaves. In front of me is a beautiful fresh plate of pancakes.

Looking around - it appears to be some time during the day, no clock, can't tell when. I can see pedestrian traffic outside the windows of the diner, and a scattering of vehicles passing by.

Everything is normal. Stable. No strangeness. I'm hungry, so it's time for pancakes. They are delicious. Am I home? Am I free?

I stared at that empty plate for longer than I care to admit. The remnants of syrup streaked across the porcelain like the faint trails of something that had been alive and now wasn't. I had eaten the pancakes. Every bite. I had drunk the coffee. I had nodded at the waitress and she had smiled and filled my cup and I had felt, for those few minutes, utterly normal.

But normal doesn't leave a green stain on your knuckles. Normal doesn't hum with the memory of a voice rising up through the stone. Normal, I was beginning to understand, was a lie the garden was very good at telling.

"You're still here."

I looked up. The woman sitting across from me in the booth—she hadn't been there a moment ago. Her hair was woven from dark purple pansies, and her eyes were two wet, glistening pebbles. She held herself with the patience of something that had been waiting a very long time.

"I don't know your name," I said. My voice came out steady, but it felt borrowed—like I'd plucked it from somewhere else and was just holding it in my mouth.

"It's not important yet," she said. "What matters is that you're starting to see. The pancakes. The diner. The street outside."

"They're not real."

"They're what you wanted them to be."

I looked down at my hands. They were clean. No moss. No blood. But when I turned them over, I saw—just for an instant—a thin, green line tracing the veins beneath my skin. The garden had not let me go. It had just dressed me in something comfortable and let me walk around inside a wish.

"How do I get out?" I asked.

"You don't get out," the woman said. "You wake up. There's a difference."

I pushed the plate aside. The clatter it made was the first real sound I'd heard since I arrived—not the soft, polite sounds of a diner, but the hard, definite sound of a thing being moved. The woman smiled. Her pebble eyes caught the light and held it.

"Now," she said, "try again."

I closed my eyes. I didn't think of pancakes this time. I thought of my study. The real study. The dust on the windowsill. The crack in the ceiling I'd always meant to patch. The weight of my old chair. The smell of paper and ink and the faint, stubborn trace of my mother's perfume, still caught in the curtains years after she'd gone.

When I opened my eyes, the diner was gone. The tilted sun was back above me, and the moss was soft beneath my feet, and the woman made of flowers was still sitting beside me, but now she sat on a low stone wall at the edge of the hedge maze.

"Good," she said. "You're learning. But you'll need more than memories. You'll need to make something the garden hasn't seen before."

I looked at the inkwell I had summoned earlier, still resting on the stone beside me. A small thing. A real thing. The first thing I had brought back from the dead world I carried inside me. I picked it up and felt its cool, familiar weight.

"Then teach me," I said.

She nodded slowly. "There's a man in the maze who knows more than I do. He's been here longer. Much longer. Find him. And whatever you do—don't eat anything he offers you."

She rose, and the hedge behind her opened into a narrow, winding path that had not been there a moment before. The tilted sun cast no shadow as she stepped into it and vanished.

I looked at Grey, still curled around my ankles. He yawned.

"Well," I said, "at least one of us isn't worried."

I picked up the inkwell and walked into the maze.

The hedge maze swallowed the sound of my footsteps. Not in the way of ordinary hedges—dirt paths and birdsong and the distant murmur of a road—but completely. The moment I stepped past the low stone wall where the flower-woman had sat, the garden's ambient stillness became something deeper. A muting. As if the maze had been built not to confuse direction but to absorb presence.

I walked with one hand brushing the hedge wall. The leaves were cool and slightly damp, and they gave under my fingers with the springy resistance of living things. But when I looked at them directly, they shimmered and became something else—not leaves, not exactly. Patterns. Repeating geometries that hurt to focus on, like trying to count the facets of a fly's eye.

I learned quickly to navigate by peripheral vision. The trick was to look slightly to the side of what I wanted to see, to let the world resolve in the edges of my sight while my direct gaze rested on nothing in particular. It was exhausting. It was also, I suspected, the point.

Grey padded ahead of me, his tail a question mark. The cat seemed untroubled by the maze's visual treachery. Cats, I remembered, don't rely on direct focus the way humans do. Their vision is already peripheral, already attuned to motion and shadow. Grey was built for this place. Or the place had been built for Grey. Either possibility unsettled me.

We walked for what felt like an hour. The tilted sun did not move. The hedges branched and turned and sometimes dead-ended into walls of solid stone, forcing us back along corridors we had already walked—or corridors that looked like ones we had walked. I began marking turns with the inkwell, dipping my finger into the ceramic well and painting small blue dots on the hedge leaves. The ink held. It was the first thing from my world that had persisted reliably in this place, and I felt an absurd gratitude toward it.

At a fork where three paths diverged—left, right, and a narrow stair leading downward into a darkness that smelled of wet roots—I stopped.

"Grey," I said. "Which way?"

The cat sat down and began washing his paw. Unhelpful. But as I waited, I became aware of something I'd been trying not to notice: a sound. Very faint. Very far. A rhythmic scraping, like a shovel turning gravel, or a heavy foot dragging across stone. It came from the left-hand path.

The flower-woman had said there was a man in the maze. Someone who knew more. She'd also said not to eat anything he offered. The scraping sound was not an invitation, exactly, but it was the only thing in the maze that felt like direction.

I went left.

The corridor narrowed as I walked. The hedges grew taller and closer together, their leaves interlocking overhead until the tilted sun was only a scatter of gold coins falling through the gaps. The scraping grew louder. Then it stopped.

I rounded a corner and found the source.

A man—or something shaped like a man—was kneeling in a small clearing, his back to me. He was digging. His hands moved with a mechanical patience, scooping dark soil from a shallow trench and piling it to one side. Beside him sat a small, covered basket. The cover was checkered cloth, red and white, the kind of cloth that belonged to picnics and normal afternoons and a world that no longer existed.

I stopped at the edge of the clearing. Grey wound between my ankles.

"You're the one she mentioned," I said.

The man did not look up. "She mentions many things. Most of them true, some of them kind, very few of them both." His voice was dry, like the rustle of old paper. "You're Ilden. I know. She told me you were coming. She tells me everything eventually—this garden has very little in the way of entertainment."

He sat back on his heels and turned to face me. His face was unremarkable except for one thing: where his eyes should have been, there were two small, dark mirrors. Not empty sockets. Reflective surfaces, perfectly round, catching my own image back at me with unsettling clarity.

"You've been remembering things into existence," he said. "That's good. That's the first skill. The second skill is not eating things into existence, which apparently she already warned you about." He gestured at the covered basket. "Don't worry. That's not for you."

"What's it for?"

"The soil. You'd be surprised what the soil in this garden will accept, if you ask it properly." He stood, brushing dirt from his hands, and studied me with his mirror-eyes. "You want me to teach you. That's why she sent you. But before I agree, I need to know one thing."

He stepped closer. Close enough that I could see myself reflected twice over, a miniature Ilden in each dark lens.

"What's the first thing you remembered? The very first thing you brought through?"

I held up the inkwell. The blue glaze caught the scattered light. "This. From my study."

He looked at it for a long moment. Then he nodded, slowly, as if confirming something to himself. "Small. Personal. Unimpressive." He almost smiled. "Good. The ones who start with empires and lost loves and grand philosophies—they don't last. You started with an inkwell. That means you might actually survive."

He turned and began walking down a path I hadn't noticed before—a narrow opening in the hedge that seemed to form as he approached it. "Come," he said over his shoulder. "Bring the cat. And try not to look at anything too directly. The maze doesn't like being stared at."

I followed him into the narrowing dark. The path bent and doubled back, but the mirror-eyed man never hesitated. He walked as if the maze were a body he'd memorized by touch—which, I supposed, it might be. The garden had no sun to track, no stars to steer by. Only the patience of a man who'd been here long enough to become part of the architecture.

Grey kept pace beside me, his tail brushing the hedge walls. The cat had taken to the maze with the quiet confidence of something that knew itself to be native. I envied him that.

After a time—impossible to say how long—the hedges opened into a small courtyard. A stone table stood at its center, surrounded by three chairs. The table was set for tea. A porcelain pot, two cups, a small pitcher of milk. The checkered cloth from the basket, now spread across the stone. The mirror-eyed man gestured for me to sit.

"You're wondering about the chairs," he said.

"I'm wondering about a lot of things."

"The third chair is for the cat." He sat, poured tea into both cups, and slid one toward me. "Grey prefers the saucer."

I looked at the tea. It was the color of old amber, and steam curled from its surface in slow, deliberate spirals. The smell was faintly floral, faintly bitter. My mother had kept a tin of tea like this in the study. She'd brewed it only on cold mornings, the kind where frost patterned the windows.

"I can smell it," I said. "That's not just memory."

"No," he agreed. "That's the garden. It borrows from you. Every scent, every color, every texture. It's been pulling from your mind since you arrived. The diner was the most it could manage without your cooperation. The pancakes, the coffee, the waitress—all yours. The garden just arranged them."

"I didn't ask for a diner."

"You didn't ask for an inkwell either, but you got one. The garden doesn't distinguish between wanting and remembering. That's the first lesson." He lifted his cup and drank, and I caught my own reflection in his eyes as he did—miniature and upside-down, a tiny Ilden suspended in dark glass. "Wanting is easy. It produces diners and pancakes and comfortable lies. Remembering is harder. It produces inkwells."

I wrapped my hand around the teacup. It was warm. Real. "So the diner was a want. The study was a memory."

"Precisely. The garden gave you what you reached for. When you reached for comfort, it gave you breakfast. When you reached for truth, it brought you back." He set his cup down and leaned forward, his mirror-eyes narrowing. "The question is: what do you want to reach for now?"

I thought about this. The tea cooled in my hands. Grey had jumped onto the third chair and was investigating the milk pitcher with the solemn focus of a creature who understood dairy.

"I want to remember my world," I said. "All of it. Not just the inkwells and the teacups. The people. The places. The things I've already started to forget."

"That's not a want," he said. "That's a decision. The garden can work with decisions. It can't work with half-measures." He stood and walked to the edge of the courtyard, where the hedge wall waited, dark and dense and patient. "Come. I'll show you something."

I followed. Grey stayed with the milk.

The mirror-eyed man pressed his hand flat against the hedge, and the leaves parted—not like a door opening, but like a curtain drawing back. Beyond the gap was not another corridor, but a room. A room I recognized.

It was my study. The real one. The dust on the windowsill was exactly as I'd left it. The crack in the ceiling ran the same course. The old chair sat behind the desk, its leather worn to the shape of my body. The pressed ferns hung in their frames. The books lined the walls, their spines a patchwork of faded colors.

But something was wrong. I stepped inside, and the air was too still. The dust did not stir. The books did not creak. The room was a perfect reproduction—every detail, every shadow—but it had no weight. No presence. It was a photograph rendered in three dimensions. A memory with no one inside it.

"It's empty," I said.

"Yes."

"Why? I remembered it. I remembered it exactly."

"Because you remembered the room," he said. "You didn't remember yourself in it. The garden can only build what you give it. You gave it a room. You didn't give it the man who sat in the chair." He turned his mirror-eyes on me, and I saw myself again—a man standing in the doorway of his own past, looking lost. "The inkwell worked because you remembered holding it. The study didn't work because you remembered it from the outside. You were looking at it, not living in it. Do you understand the difference?"

I walked to the desk. I placed my hand on the back of the old chair. The leather was cool and unyielding. It did not remember me either.

"I think so," I said. "I have to be inside the memory. Not outside it."

"Good." He stepped back through the hedge-curtain. "Then we'll try again. And this time, don't remember a thing. Remember a person. Remember the first person you ever lost."
