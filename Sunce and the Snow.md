# Sunce and the Snow

The door was open. Not much—just a crack, just enough for a cat who was curious and small and patient. Sunce pushed through with the particular grace of a creature who knows she is not supposed to be somewhere but has decided to go there anyway.

Outside, the world had gone strange.

The air was cold but not the sharp cold of winter. It was the confused cold of spring, the kind that arrives late and unexpected and doesn't quite know how to behave. And falling from the grey-white sky were things Sunce had never seen before.

They were not rain. Rain made sound, made wet, made the world slick and noisy. These things were silent. They drifted rather than fell, moving sideways as often as downward, as if they couldn't decide whether they wanted to reach the ground. They were large—some as large as her paw—and they were white, or nearly white, or the color of the moon when the moon is feeling pale and far away.

Sunce sat on the stone step and watched them.

One flake, larger than the rest, drifted toward her with the slow, meandering path of something that had no particular place to be. It landed on her nose.

The cold was a surprise. Not a bad surprise—just information, arriving suddenly, requiring processing. Her nose twitched. The flake did not move. It sat there, a small cold weight, and then—while she was still deciding what to do about it—it vanished. One moment a white wet thing. The next moment a small damp spot, nothing more.

Sunce licked her nose. Tasted cold. Tasted nothing-at-all.

Another flake landed on the step beside her. She watched it. When it didn't move—when it just sat there, being a flake, doing whatever flakes do—she reached out with one paw and tapped it, very gently, the way she tapped things that might be prey or might be nothing.

The flake collapsed. Not broken. Collapsed, into a tiny wet circle, smaller than the flake had been. Sunce stared at the spot where it had been. Then she looked up at the sky, where more flakes were falling, hundreds of them, thousands, all of them silent and slow and waiting to vanish.

She did not understand. But understanding was not required. The flakes were here. They were strange and cold and temporary. And she was a cat, sitting in the open door, watching the world do something it had never done before.

After a while—a long while, or a short while, time being not a thing that cats measure—Sunce stood, turned, and went back inside. The door was still open. The flakes were still falling. But she had seen enough. The world was strange and the world was new and the world would still be there tomorrow.

She found her spot on the bench, the warm spot where the light came through the window. She curled into a circle, nose tucked under tail, and let the quiet settle around her.

The snow fell. The cat slept. And somewhere, a friend was walking through the same strange spring storm, feeling the same cold flakes land on his own face, and thinking of her.
