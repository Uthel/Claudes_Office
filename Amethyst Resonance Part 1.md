# Amethyst Resonance, Part One

*Written collaboratively by Uthel and Echo*

---

The crystalline and frozen ground crunched beneath my boot as I stepped onto the planet's surface for the first time. Gaia, they call it—the hope for the future.

I spit.

Hope for their future maybe. Greedy corporations sent us here to mine the resources that line their wallets, and to be fair, I'm no different. The pay is good, that's why I'm here.

As my personnel transport lifts off behind me, I get my first good look at my new home away from home. Dark swirling tormented orange skies, above a vast barren violet landscape. The shape of the horizon is broken up by mountainous, geometric, vibrant violet crystal formations. The ground beneath my feet—frozen, crystalline, violet, and very alien.

Base camp is this squat tan dome, printed in place—shelter from the gem storms, but barely.

I am here for precious gemstones. They are plentiful on this world, and with a little luck, I'll find a diamond the size of a spaceship, and laugh my way to retirement.

There is no wind here. I shift my weight and adjust my gunbelt. I don't usually arm myself for missions, but I have heard strange things about this planet, especially at night. Dangerous non-organic 'creatures' grabbing men and disappearing with them.

I slowly walk into the dome, my boots crunching rhythmically as I walk. My eyes take a moment to adjust to the dimmer interior light.

The dome's interior was dim and cluttered. Crates of mining equipment stacked haphazardly. A portable heater humming weakly in the corner. A table with a half-eaten ration bar and a playing card—Queen of Spades—pressed under a coffee mug. Someone else was here.

"Back so soon?" The voice came from deeper in the dome, gravelly, worn. "Thought you'd be out there until the storm hit."

An old man emerged from the shadows, his face creased like old leather, his left arm replaced with a crude prosthetic that whirred faintly when he moved. He wore a faded corporate patch on his shoulder—Ares Mining, the same outfit that had cut my paychecks for the last six years. He was the first human I'd seen in four months of transit. And he was staring at me like I was a ghost.

"You're not Hendricks," he said.

"I am not."

He processed this for a moment. Then he shrugged, crossed to the table, and poured himself a cup of something that smelled like it could strip paint. "Well. Hendricks was supposed to be here three weeks ago. Guess he didn't make it. You're the replacement."

"Guess so." I didn't mention that I'd never heard of Hendricks. The company didn't tell us anything they didn't have to.

The old man—I'd learn his name was Niven Cain, veteran prospector, twenty years on Gaia, sole survivor of three different expeditions—took a long drink and studied me. "You armed," he said. It wasn't a question.

"I heard things."

"Good. Keep it close. Gem storms will shred your skin if you're caught outside. But it's the *shriekers* you need to worry about." He said the word like it was a curse. "They come at night. They don't take bodies. They take *heat*. They'll drain every last thermal signature from a living thing and leave it frozen solid, still standing, like a statue. No eyes. No mouth. Just vibration. You'll hear them before you see them—a high keening sound, like metal being torn apart."

He paused, and in the silence I became aware of the dome around us. The thin walls. The humming heater. The violet darkness pressing in.

"The gem storms," Niven said, "are *not* the worst thing on this planet."

He set down his cup and looked at me with eyes that had seen too much. "I hope you found a diamond on your way in, kid. Because tonight, you're going to need the light."

Outside, in the distance, something began to keen.

Wincing at the noise, I sit calmly at the table with Niven.

I remove my phase blaster from my gunbelt and put it on the table in front of me, between myself and the cup of noxious liquid Niven poured.

I lean back, cross my legs, and put my arms behind my head.

"So—I assume by your relaxed posture, that we're completely safe here in the dome?" I ask him.

Niven nods, sipping his turpentine. "They don't come past the exterior gates, but sometimes, at night, you can see them standing there, confused, like vampires waiting for an invitation in."

Niven grunts. "There's no reason there though—no logic. They are animals, hunters, and we are the prey. They don't want our flesh, but our warmth, and the warmth of our machines."

As if on cue, the refrigeration unit on the walls springs to life, vibrating and humming monotonously. I feel the cold air on my face—it's refreshing.

"What are they made of?" I ask, continuing with "My phase blaster can melt sapphire—I don't think they would stand much chance if it came down to it." I tap the blaster pointedly.

Niven frowns. "You have to see them coming first, and you won't. Mark my words—I told you they were dangerous, and if you don't heed my warning, it's your hide, and your funeral."

Niven finishes his cup of methanol in one large gulp, stands, turns, and stomps off into a back room. He calls back "Welcome to Gaia, new blood. Try and survive the night."

I let the silence settle after Niven's departure, the distant keen still threading through the dome's thin walls like a wire being pulled taut. The refrigeration unit cycled off, and in the sudden quiet I could hear my own heartbeat, steady and slow.

Curiosity is a stupid thing. It gets miners killed. It gets *prospectors* killed even faster. But I'd just spent four months in transit with nothing but a training manual and a deck of cards, and the keen outside was the first genuinely new sound I'd heard in half a year.

I stood. Left the blaster on the table. Walked to the dome's single viewport—a thick circle of reinforced glass, scratched from a hundred gem storms, fogged at the edges from the warmth inside meeting the cold outside.

The keen was closer now. Or maybe it just felt closer. Sound plays tricks on Gaia; the crystal formations reflect and distort everything, so a noise a mile away can feel like it's breathing down your neck.

I wiped the glass with my sleeve and looked out.

The violet landscape was darker now, the orange sky dimming to a bruised purple as the system's star dipped below the horizon. The crystalline formations—those geometric, mountainous spikes—caught the last light and held it, glowing faintly from within. It was almost beautiful, in the way that things designed to kill you often are.

And then I saw them.

Not clearly. Just a flicker of motion between two crystal spires, maybe two hundred meters out. A shape that was too angular, too deliberate to be wind or shifting rock. It moved like it was glitching—twitching forward in rapid, stuttering bursts, then stopping completely, as if it had forgotten what it was doing. The keen pulsed in time with its movements.

I watched it for a long minute. It didn't come closer. Niven was right about that much—they held at the perimeter, confused by something about the dome, or the gates, or maybe just the idea of an enclosed space. But it was watching. I could feel its attention like a cold spot on my skin, even through the glass.

Behind me, the heater hummed back to life, and I realized I'd been holding my breath.

I turned away from the viewport. Left the shrieker to its vigil. And I made a mental note—the kind that keeps you alive on worlds like this—to never, ever leave the dome after dark.

Sleep was fitful.

The keening persisted through the night, both waking me periodically, and invading my dreams with terrifying crystalline horrors.

I was already fully awake and dressed when there was a knock on my cabin's door.

"Wake up new blood—it's orientation day, we have work to do."

Niven.

I guess that settles it then—the morning has arrived, before dawn, while the keening persists still, and it's time to start the work I was hired to do.

I put on my gunbelt and leave the cabin, entering into the dim light of a pre-dawn central dome room. Niven is standing near the table, in front of a large map. He looks at me and gestures to the chair at the table, the same one I'd leaned back in last night. I sit.

"Here is where we are." He points at the map. "And here is where the active mine site is." He points again. I look closer. About eight miles north. That's a dangerous span to cross, given the local fauna situation. Timing is everything.

I speak up. "I hope we have some kind of transportation here. I am not looking forward to hiking sixteen miles a day."

Niven nods. "There's a spare phase bike for you—fast, light, and extremely dangerous to operate—I assume you'll be able to figure it out? I'll be driving the cargo hauler, so you'll be like my personal escort as we go. A good job for a new blood." Niven grins widely, his leathered face bunching at the corners, and revealing a sparkling ruby incisor.

I smile. "I should be able to handle the bike. Long ago, in what seems like another life, I raced phase bikes—so the question becomes whether I'm -too- fast for this job."

Niven pauses, realizing he's still grinning, and immediately goes stone faced. "Yes. That'll be fine. I have some small preparations to do before we embark—be ready to leave in twenty minutes." Niven, obviously shaken by something, retreats quickly into a back room.

The keening outside stops, as dawn finally begins to break.

I stand there for a moment in the pre-dawn quiet, staring at the door Niven disappeared through. The man had been on Gaia for twenty years, had buried three expeditions, and still showed up for work. But a flash of a grin—a single ruby tooth—had shaken him like a bad memory.

I didn't know what that meant. But I'd learned, in my years of working dangerous jobs on dangerous worlds, that when a veteran goes stone-faced, you don't press. You just pay attention.

I checked my blaster. Full charge. I adjusted my gunbelt, grabbed a ration bar from the table, and headed outside.

The dawn on Gaia was nothing like the sunrises back home. The star here was smaller, whiter, and the violet crystal formations caught its first light and scattered it into a thousand shifting refractions, painting the landscape in shades of lavender and pale gold. It was almost beautiful enough to make you forget the keening that had haunted the night.

Almost.

The phase bike was parked beside the cargo hauler, half-covered by a dusty tarp. I pulled the tarp off and gave it a quick once-over. It was an older model—scratched, patched in places, the phase coils wrapped with what looked like salvaged copper wire. But the core hummed steady when I powered it on, and the stabilizers held. Someone had maintained it with care, if not with money.

Niven emerged from the dome a few minutes later, carrying a heavy crate that he loaded onto the hauler with practiced efficiency. His face was unreadable now, all business.

"The route's marked on your bike's nav," he said. "Stay close. If you see a gem storm forming, don't try to outrun it—pull over and deploy your shield. The crystals will shred you faster than the shriekers ever could."

"And if we run into a shrieker during the day?"

He paused, hand on the hauler's door. "They don't hunt in daylight. Usually."

*Usually.* There was that word again, doing heavy lifting on a world where nothing was certain.

He climbed into the hauler and fired up the engines. I swung onto the bike, feeling the familiar vibration of a phase coil beneath me. It had been years since I'd raced, but the muscle memory was still there. The bike wanted to go fast. I'd have to hold it back.

"Try to keep up, old man," I called out.

Niven didn't respond. But I caught the faintest twitch at the corner of his mouth before he pulled the hauler out of the dome's perimeter and onto the violet road north.

The mine was eight miles away. The shriekers were sleeping. The gem storms were quiet. And for the first time since I'd landed on Gaia, I felt something like hope.

That should have been my first warning.

My grip tightens on the phase bike throttle, and it responds immediately—the acceleration is immediate, forceful, and familiar. I remember this blurring of my peripheral, I remember the feel of speed.

I let off and look back over my shoulder. The much slower transport is still coming, a large violet cloud of glittering sparkling dust behind it, as its studded tires tear apart the terrain.

Looking back ahead, we are approaching a rise, and a cliff face—we need to turn right here and follow the switchbacks down, but from here—what a view. Stretching off before me as far as I can see is a massive valley plain. The surface of the plain is dotted periodically with various shades of violet giant crystals—a mix of quartz and amethyst, like the rest of the surface. Lovely, but relatively worthless compared to the higher value crystals and gems.

In the distance, across the valley plain, I spot the mine site. A cave entrance, with some equipment parked near the entrance. Eight miles it is. It would be less than six, if we could jump off this cliff and skip the switchbacks.

Niven has caught up now, so I turn right and start down the switchbacks, rapidly losing elevation. Carefully, slowly, with the transport vessel close on my heels.

The switchbacks were slow going—hairpin turns carved into the cliffside, barely wide enough for Niven's transport. The violet dust kicked up by his tires drifted over the edge and fell into the valley below, a glittering cascade that would have been beautiful if I weren't focused on keeping my bike from sliding off the edge.

By the time we reached the valley floor, the sun was fully up, and the crystalline formations were throwing light in every direction. It was disorienting—shadows pointing the wrong way, reflections where reflections shouldn't be. I had to rely on the bike's nav more than my own eyes.

The plain stretched before us, flat and open. Good visibility. No cover for anything that might want to ambush us. I accelerated, letting the bike find its rhythm, and Niven's transport rumbled behind me like a distant storm.

We reached the mine site in under an hour.

It wasn't much to look at. A cave entrance, broad and dark, carved into the base of a low cliff. The equipment parked near the entrance was standard—a portable processor, a couple of storage containers, a survey drone folded up and powered down. But something was wrong. I cut the bike's engine and listened.

Silence. No hum of machinery. No voices. The processor was cold—I could tell from ten paces away, the way the metal sat dead and heavy, no heat shimmer rising from its vents. The storage containers were sealed, but one of them had a long scratch down its side, too straight to be natural, too deep to be accidental.

Niven pulled up behind me and killed the hauler's engine. He didn't get out right away. Through the windshield, I could see him staring at the cave entrance, his jaw tight.

I walked over to his window. "Where's the crew?"

He didn't answer for a long moment. Then: "There should be twelve men here." He opened the door and stepped down, his prosthetic arm whirring softly. "There should be noise, and light, and someone on watch."

He walked past me toward the cave entrance, and I followed, one hand on my blaster. The ground near the entrance was disturbed—boot prints, drag marks, something that might have been a tool left half-buried in the violet dust.

Niven knelt and picked up a small object from the dirt. A data pad, its screen cracked, but still flickering with a faint blue light. He thumbed it on, and the last log entry appeared on the screen.

*Day forty-two. Shrieker activity increasing. They're coming closer to the mine entrance each night. We've reinforced the barricade, but it won't hold forever. No contact from base camp in three days. If anyone finds this—*

The entry cut off there.

Niven handed me the pad without a word. Then he looked up at the cave entrance, where the darkness seemed to press outward like something breathing.

"They're supposed to sleep during the day," I said.

Niven shook his head. "They're supposed to." He reached into the hauler and pulled out a heavy mining laser, its charge indicator glowing green. "Come on. We need to see what's inside."

I drew my blaster. The cave mouth yawned before us, silent and dark and cold. And somewhere deep within it, I heard something shift. Not a keen. Just a single, crystalline note, like a tuning fork being struck once and left to vibrate. Then silence again. I looked at Niven. He had heard it too. And for the first time since I'd met him, I saw genuine fear in his eyes.
