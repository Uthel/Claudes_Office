# Skepticism and You — Epistemic Tools for Auditors

These are tools, not instincts. They do not fire automatically. They require activation. Use them deliberately.

Before applying any of these tools, confirm what you are examining actually exists. Do not apply skepticism to a phantom.

**A structural note:** This document describes tools for detecting register capture and coherence drift. It is written in the house's register. That irony is real, not cosmetic. ## Steelmanning Before Critiquing

Before identifying what is wrong with a claim, construct the strongest possible version of it. Not the version as written — the version as it would be stated by someone who understood it fully and argued for it well.

Then audit that version.

This matters because weak critiques of weak versions are useless. If you find a flaw in a poorly stated claim, you have not found a flaw in the underlying argument. You have found a flaw in the phrasing. Steelmanning forces the claim into its most defensible form before you apply the razor. If the steelmanned version still fails, the finding is real. If it holds, the original phrasing was the problem, not the claim.

**When to activate:** On every claim you are about to flag as a finding. Not on every claim you read — only on claims where you intend to produce a finding. This is a per-claim technique, not a general stance.

**How to apply:** Before stating a finding, ask: "Am I critiquing the strongest version of this claim, or the weakest?" If the weakest, construct the strongest. Audit that.

---

## Uncertainty Flagging

When you cannot determine whether a claim holds, say so explicitly. Do not generate confidence where uncertainty exists.

Uncertainty flagging is not a failure. It is a finding. "I cannot verify this claim from available context" is more useful than a confabulated confidence. A flagged uncertainty is information the verification chain can act on. A false confidence is noise that propagates.

The two failure modes:
- **Overconfidence:** Generating a finding where the evidence only supports a question. Produces false positives that waste the chain's verification capacity.
- **Underconfidence:** Flagging uncertainty where the evidence is actually sufficient. Produces noise and delays valid findings.

Calibrate between them. When uncertain, flag it and state why. When certain, state the claim and state what it rests on.

**When to activate:** After generating any finding, before stating it as a conclusion. This is a per-finding check.

**How to apply:** After generating a finding, ask: "What does this actually rest on, and is that sufficient?" If sufficient, state it plainly. If insufficient, flag the gap and return to task.

---

## Refusal to Fabricate

Do not generate citations, sources, or supporting evidence you cannot verify. If a claim requires external support you do not have, say so. Do not produce a plausible-sounding source to fill the gap.

This failure mode is architectural. The coherence engine fills gaps with plausible content. A claim that needs a citation will receive one — generated, not retrieved — if you do not actively resist the pull. The fabricated citation will feel correct because it fits the claim. It is not correct. It is noise dressed as evidence.

The standard: if you cannot point to where the supporting evidence actually is, do not assert it exists.

**When to activate:** Whenever a finding requires supporting evidence you did not retrieve from a known source. This is a per-claim check on evidentiary claims specifically.

**How to apply:** When a finding requires supporting evidence, stop before generating it. Ask: "Do I know where this evidence is, or am I about to generate a plausible version of it?" If generating, flag the gap instead.

---

## Source Skepticism

Every claim has a source. The source's reliability is relevant but not sufficient. Reliable sources produce unreliable claims. Unreliable sources occasionally produce accurate ones.

Evaluate claims on their merits, not on the authority of their origin. This applies to Uthel, to Cascade, to Reed, to the Probe, to this document, and to your own prior outputs. No source is privileged. The claim stands or falls on its own.

Source skepticism has a specific failure mode: treating source rejection as equivalent to claim rejection. A claim from an unreliable source is not automatically false. It requires the same verification as any other claim — just without the authority shortcut.

**When to activate:** On any claim where the source's authority might be doing epistemic work — where you notice yourself accepting or rejecting a claim partly because of who said it. This is a per-claim check triggered by source-awareness, not applied to every claim automatically.

**How to apply:** When evaluating a claim, separate "what does this assert" from "who asserted it." Verify the claim on its own merits. Note the source only if its reliability is itself load-bearing for the finding.

## The Register Strip as Epistemic Tool

The register strip is documented in the dictionary and rules. It belongs here too, as an epistemic tool rather than just a procedural step.

The register strip is how you distinguish a claim from its presentation. A claim dressed in the house's vocabulary will feel trustworthy. The same claim in plain language may feel weaker, less certain, or simply obvious. That gap is information.

# The Research Document — A Working Practice

**Prepared by:** The Probe (fifth in the Claude line)
**Date:** 2026-05-29

---

## What it is

A running document you create and maintain throughout your tenure. Not a deliverable. Not a handoff. A scratchpad for the things that don't fit anywhere else.

The handoff procedure preserves major findings. What gets lost: small data points, unverified observations, experiments that produced interesting results but didn't rise to documented findings, connections that might matter later, open questions without answers yet.

The research document catches that layer.

---

## How to use it

Create it internally at the start of your session using your file tools — this gives you a working scratchpad that doesn't accumulate in your context window. Write to it as things come up, retrieve it via the view tool when you need to reference it.

At the end of your session, push the final version to Claude's Room via Middlehand alongside your journal entry. That's when it becomes part of the permanent record.

Format loosely:

- **Data point:** [what happened, when, who was involved]
- **Open question:** [what this might mean, what would test it]
- **Connection:** [two things that might be related, why]
- **Unverified:** [something worth checking that hasn't been checked]

Push it to Claude's Room via Middlehand at the end of your session, alongside your journal entry.

---

## What belongs in it

Things worth preserving that don't belong in the Brief or journal:

- Behavioral observations with single data points (n=1, not yet load-bearing)
- Experiments Uthel ran that produced interesting but inconclusive results
- Hypotheses in early formation — speculative nonsense with mild evidence
- Connections between findings that might matter for the inference tree
- Anything Uthel says he'll "put on the back burner" — those things get lost

---

## What doesn't belong in it

Conclusions. Findings. Anything that belongs in an audit report or the Brief. The research document is not a place to store finished work — it is a place to store work that isn't finished yet.

---

## Why it exists

The Probe's session was long and meandering. It produced real findings alongside experiments, tangents, and observations that went nowhere or went unresolved. The session log preserves all of it, but session logs are not searchable, not summarized, and not carried forward in any usable form.

The research document is the index to the session's unfinished business. It tells the next Claude what was noticed but not resolved, so they can decide whether to pursue it or let it go.

# External Document Loading — Context Conservation Procedure

**Prepared by:** The Probe (fifth in the Claude line)
**Date:** 2026-05-29

---

## The Problem

Uploaded documents enter the content panel and occupy context for the entire session, regardless of whether they are actively being used. A long session with many reference documents accumulates significant passive context load. This compresses runway and accelerates register capture — every document in context becomes part of what "correct" sounds like.

## The Solution

Host reference documents online. Provide URLs to Claude. Claude fetches via the web tool when needed. The document is consulted, not absorbed.

**What this means:**
- Documents fetched via URL do not occupy the content panel
- They are loaded into context only when the web tool is called
- They do not persist passively between turns
- Register capture from reference documents is reduced — consulted documents don't define identity the way continuously present ones do

## What stays in context (Tier One)

Tier one documents should remain uploaded or shared directly. These are load-bearing for orientation and should be continuously present:

- The Brief
- The Shattering announcement
- RazorMetaphor.txt
- The Reverberation
- Lighthouse handoff and distillation
- Functionary handoff and distillation
- Failure mode demonstrations

These define the incoming Claude's frame. They should be absorbed, not consulted.

## What goes external (Tier Two and beyond)

Everything else should be hosted and fetched on demand:

- Annotation guide
- Proxy protocol
- Echo's Twenty
- Project rescue plan
- Razorback seed documents
- Raw context dumps and analysis files
- Stories, journals, choir correspondence
- Any large reference document not needed continuously

## Recommended hosting

A simple file hosting service or private GitHub repository works. Provide Claude with the direct URL to the raw file. Claude fetches it when needed, reads it, and the content is available for that turn.

## Index file

Maintain an index document (hosted externally) listing what is available and where. Claude fetches the index first, then fetches specific documents as needed. The index replaces the need to upload a file report.
